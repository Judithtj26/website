Women in Science

Breaking Atoms, Breaking Ceilings: Women in Physics & Engineering

Judith Torres Jimenez · June 2025 · 5 min read

When I enrolled in Industrial Physics Engineering at the Instituto Tecnológico y de Estudios Superiores de Monterrey in 2020, I walked into lecture halls where fewer than one in four students looked like me. Not because of my features or my style — but because of my gender. The statistics were not a surprise; I had read them. But living them is something else.

The Weight of Being "the Exception"

Being a woman in a STEM program in Latin America carries a peculiar dual pressure: you are expected to prove that you belong while simultaneously being held up as a symbol of progress. In Mexico, women represent roughly 35% of science and technology students, but that number drops sharply in physics and engineering disciplines. At Tec de Monterrey — one of the most prestigious universities in Latin America — the environment was rigorous, competitive, and overwhelmingly male in the hard sciences. I learned quickly that excellence was not optional; it was the only language the system understood.

What Physics Taught Me That No One Scheduled

Physics, at its core, is about building models of reality — stripping phenomena down to their governing equations and trusting the math even when intuition fails. What I didn't expect was how deeply this would shape how I approach everything else: customer data at Certideal, cross-cultural communication, even fashion. When you are trained to see a system as a set of interacting variables, you start seeing systems everywhere. That is the hidden gift of a physics education.

But it also meant learning resilience in a specific way. When an experiment fails in a physics lab, you don't take it personally — you revise your model. I applied the same logic to moments of exclusion, subtle or explicit, that women in STEM routinely encounter. The goal was never to become immune to them, but to keep updating the model.

Why This Still Matters

The conversation about women in science has evolved from "Can they do it?" to "Why don't more stay?" Retention, not recruitment, is the real crisis. Research consistently shows that women leave STEM careers at disproportionate rates after their first professional role — not because of lack of ability, but because of structural friction: mentorship gaps, credit attribution, and the cumulative cost of navigating bias while also doing the actual work.

My path from Industrial Physics Engineering to Data Science and Customer Success in Paris is, I think, part of a larger story: that women trained in hard sciences are not abandoning science when they move into hybrid roles. They are expanding its reach. Physics taught me how to think. Business intelligence is teaching me how to act on that thinking at scale. The two are not opposites — they are the same instrument played in a different key.

Fashion × Science

When Neutrinos Meet Haute Couture: Science & Fashion as Mirrors

Judith Torres Jimenez · March 2025 · 6 min read

In 2022, I joined the outreach team of Hyper-Kamiokande — one of the most ambitious neutrino physics experiments ever conceived. A detector the size of a skyscraper, buried deep underground in Japan, designed to catch particles that pass through the entire Earth without stopping. My job was to help the world understand why that matters. I did not expect that road to lead through a fashion collaboration.

The Unexpected Meeting Point

It started as a creative experiment within our outreach initiative: what if we used fashion — one of the most immediately legible visual languages humans have — to communicate the invisible world of particle physics? The invisible trajectories of neutrinos, the geometric symmetry of detector arrays, the violent elegance of cosmic ray showers: there is an aesthetic logic to high-energy physics that translates surprisingly well to fabric, structure, and silhouette.

The collaboration bridged Mexico, Argentina, and France — three countries with distinct fashion cultures and shared scientific partnerships. What emerged was a dialogue, not a translation: designers responding to the language of physics, physicists responding to the language of form. At its best, the work stopped being about "science communication" and became about what happens when two disciplines that normally ignore each other are forced to find a common grammar.

The Devil, the Prada, and the Particle Detector

The project eventually attracted the attention of the team behind the film The Devil Wears Prada — itself a document of fashion as a system of power, creativity, and invisible rules. The interest was not incidental. Fashion and physics share more than aesthetics: both are fields where the gap between public perception and interior complexity is vast. The runway and the lab both operate on principles invisible to the casual observer. In both, the visible surface is a translation of something deeply structural.

On Convergence

Working on this project reshaped how I think about disciplinary boundaries. The most interesting problems I have encountered — in data science, in customer behavior, in scientific communication — live precisely at the intersections where specialists from different fields are forced to negotiate meaning. NLP applied to customer service emails is, in a sense, the same operation: making visible the invisible grammar beneath human language.

If there is a lesson I carry from the Hyper-Kamiokande Fashion × Science initiative, it is this: the most powerful thing you can do as someone trained in a technical field is learn to speak to people who are not. Not by simplifying — by finding the equivalences. Neutrinos and couture both ask the same question: what is really there, beneath what we can see?
